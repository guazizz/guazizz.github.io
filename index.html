📖 Robert Frost & Context

Robert Frost (1874–1963)
American poet · Famous for poems about rural New England · Won 4 Pulitzer Prizes

📅 "Mending Wall" – Published 1914 in North of Boston

Frost often wrote about everyday life, but his poems hide deeper questions. He liked to show two sides of an argument without picking one.

🧱 What's the poem about? Two neighbours meet each spring to fix the stone wall between their properties. The speaker thinks the wall is pointless. The neighbour just repeats: “Good fences make good neighbours.”

🎯 Key idea: The wall is real, but it also stands for the barriers we build between people — habits, traditions, and beliefs we follow without thinking.

📜 Lines 1–4

“Something there is that doesn’t love a wall,
That sends the frozen-ground-swell under it,
And spills the upper boulders in the sun,
And makes gaps even two can pass abreast.”
🔍 What's happening: The poem starts with a mystery. Some force — maybe nature — hates the wall. Frost uses personification (giving human feelings to nature). The wall gets broken by frost, ground movement, and gravity.

💡 Why it matters: The speaker suggests the wall is unnatural. Nature keeps trying to tear it down. “Even two can pass abreast” — the gaps are big enough for two people to walk through side by side. Maybe nature wants people to connect, not be separated.
“The work of hunters is another thing:”
🔍 Quick note: Hunters also break the wall, but for a different reason — to flush out rabbits. The speaker has to fix their damage too.

📜 Lines 12–16

“I let my neighbour know beyond the hill;
And on a day we meet to walk the line
And set the wall between us once again.
We keep the wall between us as we go.
To each the boulders that have fallen to each.”
🔍 What's happening: Every spring, they meet to fix the wall. It's like a ritual. But notice: they “keep the wall between us” even while working together. They stay on their own sides.

💡 Why it matters: The wall isn't just stone — it's a symbol of how people keep distance between each other. Even when cooperating, they don't really connect. Frost shows the wall creates separation, not friendship.

📜 Lines 17–23

“And some are loaves and some so nearly balls
We have to use a spell to make them balance:
‘Stay where you are until our backs are turned!’
We wear our fingers rough with handling them.
Oh, just another kind of outdoor game,
One on a side. It comes to little more:”
🔍 What's happening: The stones are oddly shaped — some like loaves of bread, some like balls. They joke and use a “spell” to make them stay. The speaker calls the whole thing “just another kind of outdoor game.”

💡 Why it matters: The speaker mocks the wall. He thinks it's pointless — just a game. The humour shows his attitude: he doesn't take the wall seriously. But the neighbour does.
“There where it is we do not need the wall:
He is all pine and I am apple orchard.”
🔍 What's happening: The speaker gives a logical reason: his apple trees and the neighbour's pine trees won't mix. They don't need a wall.
💡 Why it matters: The speaker represents questioning and common sense. He sees no danger — so why keep the wall?

📜 Lines 24–27

“My apple trees will never get across
And eat the cones under his pines, I tell him.
He only says, ‘Good fences make good neighbours.’”
🔍 What's happening: The speaker makes a funny joke — apple trees don't eat pine cones! He's being playful, trying to make the neighbour see how silly the wall is.

💡 Why it matters: The neighbour's reply is short: “Good fences make good neighbours.” He doesn't argue. He just repeats the old saying. This shows the difference between them: one thinks, the other just follows tradition. The neighbour won't change his mind.

📜 Lines 30–34

“‘Why do they make good neighbours? Isn’t it
Where there are cows? But here there are no cows.
Before I built a wall I’d ask to know
What I was walling in or walling out,
And to whom I was like to give offence.”
🔍 What's happening: The speaker imagines what he'd like to say to his neighbour. He argues that walls only make sense if you have cows that might wander. But here, there are no cows. So why have a wall?

💡 Why it matters: This is the heart of the poem. The speaker asks the big question: “What I was walling in or walling out?” Frost wants us to ask ourselves the same thing. What barriers do we build without thinking? Who are we keeping out — or keeping in?

📜 Lines 35–38

“Something there is that doesn’t love a wall,
That wants it down.’ I could say ‘Elves’ to him,
But it’s not elves exactly, and I’d rather
He said it for himself.”
🔍 What's happening: The speaker repeats the opening line — nature wants the wall down. He jokes that he could say elves did it, but that's not true. More importantly, he wishes the neighbour would figure this out on his own.

💡 Why it matters: The speaker doesn't want to force his ideas. He wants the neighbour to think for himself. But the neighbour won't.

📜 Lines 38–42

“I see him there,
Bringing a stone grasped firmly by the top
In each hand, like an old-stone savage armed.
He moves in darkness as it seems to me,
Not of woods only and the shade of trees.”
🔍 What's happening: The speaker watches his neighbour carry stones. He uses a simile: “like an old-stone savage armed” — like a caveman holding weapons. Then he says the neighbour “moves in darkness.”

💡 Why it matters: This is harsh. “Darkness” doesn't mean literal dark — it means the neighbour is stuck in old thinking. He won't question anything. The caveman image makes him seem primitive, unchanging. The speaker feels frustrated.

📜 Lines 43–45 (End)

“He will not go behind his father’s saying,
And he likes having thought of it so well
He says again, ‘Good fences make good neighbours.’”
🔍 What's happening: The poem ends with the neighbour repeating the proverb one last time. He won't question what his father said. He thinks he's being clever, but really he's just copying.

💡 Why it matters: The wall stays. The neighbour doesn't change. Frost doesn't give us an answer — he leaves it open. The question hangs in the air: should we keep building walls just because we always have?

🏁 What Frost Leaves Us With: No winner. But the speaker's question stays with us: “Before I built a wall I'd ask to know what I was walling in or walling out.” That's the poem's message — think before you build barriers.

📌 Summary & Key Quotes

⚡ Main Ideas

  • Tradition vs questioning: The neighbour repeats his father; the speaker asks “why?”
  • Walls separate people: Even working together, they stay apart.
  • Nature wants walls down: The “something” that doesn't love a wall is nature itself.
  • Think for yourself: The poem challenges us to examine our own barriers.

📜 Top Quotes to Remember

  • 🔹 “Something there is that doesn’t love a wall” — nature vs human division.
  • 🔹 “Good fences make good neighbours” — the old saying, repeated.
  • 🔹 “like an old-stone savage armed” — neighbour as stubborn, primitive.
  • 🔹 “Before I built a wall I’d ask to know / What I was walling in or walling out” — the poem's central question.

💭 Think About: Are there “walls” in your life — habits, beliefs, boundaries — that you keep without asking why? Frost wants us to question them.
