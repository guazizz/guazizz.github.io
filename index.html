Mending Wall

Something there is that doesn’t love a wall,
That sends the frozen-ground-swell under it,
And spills the upper boulders in the sun,
And makes gaps even two can pass abreast.
The work of hunters is another thing:
I have come after them and made repair
Where they have left not one stone on a stone,
But they would have the rabbit out of hiding,
To please the yelping dogs. The gaps I mean,
No one has seen them made or heard them made,
But at spring mending-time we find them there.
I let my neighbour know beyond the hill;
And on a day we meet to walk the line
And set the wall between us once again.
We keep the wall between us as we go.
To each the boulders that have fallen to each.
And some are loaves and some so nearly balls
We have to use a spell to make them balance:
‘Stay where you are until our backs are turned!’
We wear our fingers rough with handling them.
Oh, just another kind of outdoor game,
One on a side. It comes to little more:
There where it is we do not need the wall:
He is all pine and I am apple orchard.
My apple trees will never get across
And eat the cones under his pines, I tell him.
He only says, ‘Good fences make good neighbours.’
Spring is the mischief in me, and I wonder
If I could put a notion in his head:
‘Why do they make good neighbours? Isn’t it
Where there are cows? But here there are no cows.
Before I built a wall I’d ask to know
What I was walling in or walling out,
And to whom I was like to give offence.
Something there is that doesn’t love a wall,
That wants it down.’ I could say ‘Elves’ to him,
But it’s not elves exactly, and I’d rather
He said it for himself. I see him there,
Bringing a stone grasped firmly by the top
In each hand, like an old-stone savage armed.
He moves in darkness as it seems to me,
Not of woods only and the shade of trees.
He will not go behind his father’s saying,
And he likes having thought of it so well
He says again, ‘Good fences make good neighbours.’

📖 Context & Overview

Robert Frost (1874–1963) · American poet · Writes about farms and nature · Published 1914 in North of Boston

Two neighbours meet each spring to fix a stone wall. The speaker asks why they need it. The neighbour just says: “Good fences make good neighbours.”

⚡ Main Conflict

The poem looks at old ways vs new ideas. Should we keep doing things just because we always have? Frost wants us to think about why we put up walls.

📅 Published: 1914 (North of Boston)

🧱 Main idea: The wall is real but also stands for how we keep people out.

Presented by: Philip, Chris Honor 8-3

🗣️ The Speaker vs. The Neighbour

🌿 The Speaker

Asks questions · playful · makes jokes · thinks wall is useless · wants to connect

“Something there is that doesn’t love a wall”

“My apple trees will never get across / And eat the cones” — funny idea

Wants to “put a notion in his head” — make him think

🪨 The Neighbour

Sticks to old ways · stubborn · just repeats what his dad said · wants to stay apart

“Good fences make good neighbours” (twice)

“like an old-stone savage armed” — like a caveman, won't change

“He moves in darkness” — won't think for himself

🧩 What It Means

The neighbour just follows tradition. The speaker thinks for himself. But the wall stays up — Frost doesn't say who is right.

🎭 Key Themes

ThemeExplanation & Evidence
Old Ways vs New IdeasNeighbour: “will not go behind his father’s saying” — just repeats. Speaker wants to ask why.
Walls Between PeopleWall shows separation: “We keep the wall between us as we go” — even when working together, they stay apart.
Nature vs Human WallsNature breaks the wall: “frozen-ground-swell” makes gaps. Nature seems to want it down.
Think for Yourself vs Just FollowSpeaker thinks; neighbour just repeats old sayings without thinking.

💡 Frost doesn't say who wins — he wants us to ask: what are we walling in or walling out?

✍️ Language & Structure

🔠 Language Tricks

  • Personification: “Something there is that doesn’t love a wall” — nature acts like it hates walls.
  • Simile: “like an old-stone savage armed” — neighbour is like a caveman, stuck in the past.
  • Metaphor: “darkness” — means he won't think or question things.
  • Humour: “My apple trees will never get across / And eat the cones” — makes the wall seem silly.

📐 How the Poem is Built

  • Blank verse (unrhymed iambic pentameter) — sounds like normal talking.
  • Repetition: First line comes back later. The proverb is said twice — shows the neighbour won't change.
  • Enjambment: Lines run on without stops — shows the speaker's mind is active.
  • Like a monologue: We see the neighbour through the speaker's eyes.
⭐ Key point: No ending — the wall is still there. The argument never ends.

🏁 Conclusion & Key Quotes

What Frost Leaves Us With: No one wins. The wall stays. The neighbour doesn't change. But the speaker makes us think: “Before I built a wall I’d ask to know / What I was walling in or walling out.” The poem asks us to look at our own walls.

📜 Top 4 Quotes

  • 🔹 “Something there is that doesn’t love a wall” — nature wants walls down.
  • 🔹 “Good fences make good neighbours” — the old saying the neighbour keeps saying.
  • 🔹 “He moves in darkness as it seems to me” — he doesn't think for himself.
  • 🔹 “Before I built a wall I’d ask to know / What I was walling in or walling out” — the big question of the poem.

Are there walls in your life — habits, beliefs, boundaries — that you keep without asking why?
